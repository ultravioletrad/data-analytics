IPP Capacity Payments and the Electricity Paradox

You pay for the plant.
You pay for the debt.
Then you tolerate the hottest weather with random electricity outages in peak summer and winter.

Pakistan has 46.2 GW of installed electricity capacity. Baseload demand runs at 12,000 to 12,500 MW. Over half the installed capacity sits idle most of the year. Yet consumers pay capacity charges for every megawatt whether it generates electricity or not, fund the circular debt those payments created, and then endure hours of loadshedding on top of it all.

The structure of the problem: Under take-or-pay Power Purchase Agreements signed under the 1994, 2002 and 2015 power policies, the government guaranteed capacity payments to IPPs regardless of actual electricity generation. 71% of the electricity tariff is capacity charge. Only 29% is for actual energy consumed. CPEC coal plants, which carry the highest capacity payments and lowest utilisation factors, are dollar-indexed, meaning every rupee depreciation since signing automatically increased the rupee cost to consumers.

The NEPRA oversight failure: "Currently, all generation plants are designed with capacity payments, but there is hardly any monitoring of actual capacity and availability. There is no verification of IPP power supply claims." (PIDE, 2024). The Central Power Purchasing Agency (CPPA-G) has never scrutinised IPP invoicing, neither in the past nor currently. The Power Sector Inquiry Report 2020 found irregularities, ordered a full audit. It never happened.

The core paradox: Pakistan has 46.2 GW of installed generation capacity. Baseload demand is 12,000 to 12,500 MW. Over half the contracted capacity sits idle most of the year yet earns full capacity payments. After Kot Addu Power Company's retirement, take-or-pay installed capacity still exceeds 41,000 MW. Consumers pay for this idle capacity through a tariff where 71 paisa of every rupee is a capacity charge, before a single unit of electricity is generated or consumed.

Then they tolerate the hottest weather with random electricity outages in peak summer and winter. Despite this installed surplus, loadshedding continues across Pakistan in both summer and winter, in urban and rural areas. The contradiction is not accidental. It is structural: the wrong plants are contracted, transmission bottlenecks prevent power from reaching where it is needed, and economically efficient plants are curtailed while expensive ones run because of contractual obligations.

The dollar indexation trap: CPEC coal power plants, which hold the highest share of capacity payments and the lowest utilisation factors, have their contracts indexed to the US dollar. The 1994 power policy similarly guaranteed dollar-denominated returns to attract foreign investment. Every depreciation of the rupee since signing has automatically inflated the rupee cost of capacity payments to consumers. This exchange rate risk was transferred entirely to Pakistani households and businesses at the point of contract signing, without public knowledge or consent.

The private sector narrative obscures that the state is paying itself capacity charges for inefficient plants it owns.

The government pays itself, and still cannot afford it: 52% of installed generation capacity is government-owned. These government plants (Gencos) receive the same capacity payment structure as private IPPs. Many Gencos built in the 1980s have exceeded their operational lives, run at low efficiency, and should have been retired. Instead they remain in the system, drawing capacity payments. Their workforces are idle. The fiscal constraint from paying capacity charges to its own inefficient plants is one reason the government cannot fund development spending. 43% of the federal budget goes to debt servicing, partly because power sector debt compounds through the circular debt mechanism.

Winter loadshedding is not a capacity shortage. It is a system failure. In January 2026, Pakistan faced a 4,000 MW shortfall despite 46.2 GW of installed capacity. The reason: fog caused plant tripping in the south, the Matiari-Lahore HVDC transmission line was affected, gas supply to Lahore-based power plants was curtailed, and LNG supply from Qatar was suspended. The plants exist. The transmission grid cannot move power from south to north efficiently. Overloaded 500 kV lines force curtailment of economic base-load plants and reliance on costlier RLNG plants when available. Consumers paid capacity charges for all of it regardless.

Summer loadshedding has a different cause but the same result. Peak demand hits 27 to 30 GW. Hydel runs at capacity. Thermal plants are dispatched by economic merit order, but transmission congestion in the south prevents full dispatch of coal and nuclear plants. System operators must curtail cheap generation and run expensive RLNG in the north. The consumer pays both the higher energy price and the capacity charge for the curtailed plant.

The circular debt chain: how costs layer onto the bill
1
Capacity payments issued to IPPs and Gencos
₨1.9T in FY24, regardless of utilisation. Dollar-indexed CPEC plants receive highest per-unit payments at lowest output. Government cannot afford to pay on time.
2
Unpaid dues accumulate as circular debt
₨2.4T circular debt stock in FY24, equal to 24% of total tax revenue. CPPA-G owes GENCOs, GENCOs owe fuel suppliers, fuel suppliers cut supply, plants cannot run. The chain of non-payment causes supply disruption.
3
Government borrows to plug the gap
Power Holding Private Limited (PHPL) borrows on behalf of the power sector. Interest on PHPL debt alone constitutes a line item in circular debt. This borrowing adds to the domestic debt stack, the same T-bills the JazzCash campaign promotes as investment.
4
Interest and debt passed to consumers via surcharges
The Debt Service Surcharge (₨2.84/unit in FY25) appears as a line item on electricity bills to repay PHPL debt. Circular debt interest charges (IPPs and PHL) are a separate component embedded in the tariff. Fuel Cost Adjustments and Quarterly Tariff Adjustments add further variability.
5
PDL and gas levies complete the extraction
The Petroleum Development Levy on fuel used to run thermal power plants is paid by consumers indirectly through higher energy charges. Gas levies (GIDC) similarly feed through to generation costs. The consumer funds the fuel, funds the capacity, funds the debt on the capacity, and funds the interest on that debt. All in a single monthly bill..
6
Loadshedding despite full payment
Having paid for all of the above, the consumer receives interrupted supply. No refund mechanism exists for hours not supplied. The 1,000 high-loss feeder areas receive 12 to 16 hours of scheduled outages as a policy matter to deter theft, punishing all consumers in those areas including those who pay in full.

The oversight that never happened: The Power Sector Inquiry Report 2020 found irregularities in IPP invoicing: "most issues stem from inaccurate invoicing by IPPs and misinterpreting PPAs." A full operational and financial audit of all IPPs was ordered. It was never carried out. NEPRA sets tariffs but does not review or approve PPAs. CPPA-G (the single buyer) has never scrutinised IPP invoices. The October 2024 termination of five IPP contracts is projected to save ₨411 billion, meaningful, but against a ₨1.9T annual capacity payment bill and ₨2.4T circular debt stock, the relief is marginal without structural reform of the procurement model, transmission investment, and regulatory independence.

Renegotiation has limits the government will not state publicly. Pakistan has lost international arbitrations over IPP contract disputes before, with Hubco in the 1990s being the most prominent. Every PPA renegotiation that goes to arbitration costs foreign exchange Pakistan cannot afford. This is the constraint that makes reform slow and partial. The consumer absorbs the cost of a system the government cannot renegotiate, cannot afford, and cannot explain.

Summer peak demand: why 46 GW is never enough when you need it

The question every Pakistani asks in June, July and August: if we have 46.2 GW of installed capacity and we pay for all of it, why is there no electricity when temperatures hit 45 degrees Celsius and demand peaks?

The answer is not a shortage of plants. It is a failure of the system connecting them to homes. Peak summer demand reaches 27,000 to 30,000 MW. Hydel generation drops as glacial melt does not compensate for reservoir drawdown from the previous season. Thermal plants in the south are constrained by the transmission network. The 500 kV grid linking southern generation to northern load centres operates near its physical limit. When it does, the system operator has two choices: curtail generation in the south or risk cascading tripping across the whole grid. They curtail. Punjab, KPK and Azad Kashmir absorb the cuts even though the plants they are paying for through capacity charges are physically running in Sindh and Balochistan.

RLNG plants in the north, which could serve Punjab directly, depend on LNG imports. When global LNG prices spiked in 2022, Pakistan could not afford spot cargoes. When Qatar paused supply in early 2026, northern plants lost fuel. The installed capacity number on paper bears no relationship to what a household in Lahore, Faisalabad or Peshawar can actually receive on a peak summer afternoon. Consumers pay capacity charges for southern coal plants they cannot access and fuel levies for northern gas plants that have no gas.

Why the system cannot simply dispatch more electricity in a heatwave: Three simultaneous constraints bind the system at peak. First, hydel output falls in early summer before monsoon inflows restore reservoirs, reducing the cheapest generation available. Second, transmission congestion in the south prevents full dispatch of coal plants even when they are available and contracted. Third, RLNG availability in the north depends on spot LNG procurement the government cannot always afford, particularly after the foreign exchange crises of 2022 to 2023.

Demand-side factors worsen each year. Air conditioning penetration is rising fast as incomes grow and temperatures increase. A household that ran two fans in 2015 now runs one or two air conditioners in 2025. System peak is growing faster than transmission capacity is being added. The government approved Rs 660 billion in transmission upgrades in 2023 but execution has lagged. Meanwhile, consumers pay for the capacity that exists on paper and receive cuts in the heat because the wire to carry that power does not exist or cannot carry the load.

Industrial production in summer is directly affected. Textile exporters, cold chain operators and food processors face the highest electricity bills in the system, fund the most capacity through tariff cross-subsidy, and lose the most production hours to unscheduled cuts in the months when they most need to run cooling and processing equipment. This is a direct drag on export earnings and on the same foreign exchange Pakistan needs to service its external debt.

The solar exit and what it means for remaining consumers

Those who can afford to leave are leaving. Net-metered solar capacity reached 4.9 GW by March 2025, nearly double the 1.3 GW in FY23. Over 293,000 consumers have installed rooftop solar. Electricity sales dropped 2.8% YoY in FY24, the second consecutive year of decline despite GDP growing 2.4%. Industrial consumers dropped 11% in electricity purchases. Agriculture dropped 11%.

This creates a tariff death spiral for remaining consumers. As higher-income households and industries exit the grid, the fixed capacity payment burden (₨1.9T per year) must be recovered from a shrinking base of consumers who cannot afford solar. NEPRA raises tariffs to recover fixed costs. Higher tariffs push more consumers to solar. The remaining consumers, mostly lower-income rural households unable to invest in solar, bear an ever-larger share of the fixed capacity cost. The system is cannibalising its own paying consumer base.
