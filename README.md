## Published analyses

### The T-bill Illusion and the Debt Trap
*June 2026*

VEON's JazzCash and Mobilink Bank launched government treasury bills via
smartphone, calling it "democratising investment for millions of Pakistanis."
The official SBP and Ministry of Finance data tells a different story.

Pakistan ran a primary surplus of ₨1.8T in FY-25 — meaning revenues covered
all operational spending. Yet it still borrowed ₨7.1T more, purely to service
₨9.46T in interest on past debt. The new borrowing was not for roads, schools
or hospitals. It was to pay the banks and bondholders already owed.

Key findings:
- Interest expense FY-25: ₨9.46T (SBP Statistical Bulletin, Table 5.2)
- 43% of the entire federal budget goes to debt servicing
- Commercial banks hold 64% of domestic debt via closed primary auctions
- JazzCash retail participation: statistically ~0% of the debt stock
- External debt servicing peaked at 60% of exports in FY-23, breaching the
  World Bank's 40% elevated-risk threshold
- The primary surplus was achieved by compressing provincial transfers and
  extracting levies via PDL, electricity surcharges and gas tariffs — not
  through economic growth

### IPP Capacity Payments — The ₨1.8 Trillion Liability
*June 2026*

PMLN claims to have ended loadshedding, yet Pakistan faces hours of power cuts
while paying billions for idle capacity. PIDE data reveals the scale of the
crisis.

Key findings:
- Capacity payments exceeded ₨1.77T in FY22, projected to reach ₨2.8T by FY25
- 71% of the power purchase price goes to capacity payments, only 29% for energy
- IPPs are paid even when plants sit idle — no verification of supply claims
- Agreements under the 1994 Power Policy are dollar-denominated, exposing
  Pakistan to exchange rate risks
- "Hardly any monitoring" of actual capacity and availability, according to PIDE
