The T-bill Illusion: Pakistan's Domestic Debt Burden, Pakistan is borrowing to pay
interest on borrowing

Pakistan ran a primary surplus of ₨1.8T in FY-25, meaning government revenues covered all operational spending without any borrowing. Yet it still ran a ₨7.1T fiscal deficit and issued fresh debt. Why? Because ₨9.46T in interest on past debt had to be paid. Every new T-bill issued today finances yesterday's interest bill, not a single road, school, or hospital.

The structural reality: Pakistan's deficit is no longer a governance rsource constraint problem. It is entirely a debt-servicing problem, the compounding cost of decades of borrowing. A catchy JazzCash digital launch does not change this. It recruits retail savers into a system that extracts from them twice: once as taxpayers who fund the interest, and again as "investors" who get secondary-market access after banks lock in primary auction rates.

Meanwhile, external debt servicing breached the World Bank's 40% of exports danger threshold, peaking at 60% of exports in FY-23 (33% when remittances are included), still sitting at 38–39% today. There are internationally recognised limits on how much a government should borrow relative to its foreign exchange earnings. Pakistan has been near or above these limits for years without public scrutiny or political accountability.

Interest is the engine of debt growth

In FY-25, a ₨1.8T primary surplus partially offset the debt increase, but ₨8.9T in interest payments still drove debt stock up ₨9.2T. The surplus helped at the margin. It could not stop the compounding.

Primary surplus is real but insufficient

At ₨1.8T it is 5× smaller than the interest it must offset. Pakistan needs sustained primary surpluses far larger than ₨1.8T to stabilise the debt ratio.

Source: Annual Debt Review FY-25, Debt Policy Coordination Office, Ministry of Finance, p.9. Chart shows annual changes in debt stock by driver. Interest expense and primary balance are flow variables; FX/revaluation effects are stock adjustments. These are not netted against each other.

If Pakistan has a primary surplus, why borrow at all? Because the ₨9.46T interest bill on accumulated past debt turns a ₨1.8T operational surplus into a ₨7.1T deficit. The government did not need to borrow for schools, salaries or infrastructure. It borrowed to pay the banks and bondholders it already owes. This is the definition of a debt trap, and issuing more T-bills, whether through a bank auction room or a smartphone app, deepens it.

What limits exist? The World Bank flags an external debt service-to-exports ratio above 40% as elevated risk, this measures foreign currency debt repayment against export earnings only. Pakistan hit 60% on this measure in FY-23 (33% when remittances are added). It remains at 38–39% today, near that danger line, while launching retail debt campaigns with no public acknowledgement of where the proceeds go or what the sustainable borrowing limit is.

How was the primary surplus achieved?

Not through growth but through compression. The government achieved this surplus by squeezing provincial transfers and compressing development spending, not through high GDP growth. Under a fragile recovery with interest rates above 20% and inflation near 25%, the surplus was a policy choice to compress provincial funds, not a sign of economic strength. This is why the surplus exists alongside a massive interest bill and continued borrowing — the govt did not grow its way to fiscal health, it cut transfers to provinces to show a number.

Where the primary surplus actually comes from

The surplus is extracted from the public, not earned through growth. Beyond squeezing provincial transfers, a significant part of government revenue feeding the primary surplus comes from levies and surcharges passed directly onto consumers:

PDL (Petroleum Development Levy) is a fixed rupee charge per litre of fuel collected at the pump regardless of income. Not a production tax, not a business tax. A direct extraction from every household and business that uses transport or energy. The FY-25 PDL target alone was ₨1,281 billion.

Electricity bill surcharges, consumers pay multiple embedded charges including the Debt Service Surcharge (DSS) which repays power sector borrowing, and capacity payments to IPPs (Independent Power Producers) under take-or-pay contracts. These contracts require Pakistan to pay IPPs for capacity whether electricity is used or not. In recent bills, a separate line item for circular debt interest repayments has also appeared. These are not electricity costs. They are debt repayments and guaranteed corporate profits billed as utility charges.

Natural gas levies GIDC (Gas Infrastructure Development Cess) and other surcharges similarly extracted and used partly to service gas sector debt.

All three are regressive, they fall per unit consumed, not per rupee of income. A daily wage worker and a factory owner pay the same PDL per litre. The burden as a share of income falls hardest on those least able to bear it. This is the real composition of Pakistan's primary surplus.

The interest rate trap, how SBP rate hikes fed the same cycle: When the State Bank raised the policy rate above 20% to fight inflation, the direct consequence was that government T-bill and PIB yields rose in lockstep — making the government's own interest bill larger. More interest expense meant a deeper deficit, which required more T-bill issuance, which crowded out private credit, which raised the cost of production for every business, which raised retail prices across the economy, the very inflation the rate hike was meant to suppress.

The correct lever was never the interest rate alone. Reducing PDL, removing capacity payment pass-throughs from electricity bills, cutting circular debt interest surcharges, and reducing domestic interest expense directly would have lowered the cost of energy and transport across the economy — disinflating from the supply side without requiring a 20%+ policy rate that crushed private investment, deepened the government's own debt trap, and increased costs of production simultaneously. The SBP was fighting a fire the fiscal side was actively feeding.

Every rupee of domestic interest expense reduced is a rupee that does not need to be extracted from consumers via levies, tariffs or fresh borrowing. The debt trap and the inflation trap are the same trap.

40% World Bank danger threshold

Pakistan has been breaching internationally recognised borrowing limits without public scrutiny. The World Bank flags a debt service-to-exports ratio above 40% as elevated risk. Measured against exports alone, Pakistan peaked at 60% in FY-23, meaning 60 cents of every export dollar went to external debt repayment that year. Measured against exports plus remittances (a more complete picture of Pakistan's foreign exchange inflows, since remittances run ~$27B annually), the peak was 33%, still very close to the threshold. Today the exports-only ratio sits at 38–39%, still hugging the danger line. The position has improved from the FY-23 crisis peak, but remains elevated, and critically, this chart captures only external debt servicing in foreign currency. It does not include the ₨9.46T domestic interest burden which falls entirely on Pakistani taxpayers and utility bill payers. Both burdens are real and simultaneous. Yet the government launches smartphone T-bill campaigns with no public acknowledgement of either constraint.

The primary dealer system: T-bill auctions are conducted through primary dealers or commercial banks that bid directly with the government in a closed room, locking in institutional rates. The public is excluded from this process. JazzCash gives retail customers secondary-market access after banks have already priced the instrument. NSS has offered retail government securities for decades at low returns. JazzCash adds a fintech fee layer and a Finance Minister press conference.

What the launch actually provides: A NASDAQ-listed multinational earns fees on Pakistani citizens buying government debt, after commercial banks locked in primary auction pricing. NSS already does this without the fintech layer while the Govt gives lower returns. The public is recruited into the debt machine twice: as taxpayers who fund ₨9.46T in annual interest, and as retail "investors" who receive whatever yield remains after banks have taken their position.

The purchasing power connection: Every rupee of unnecessary government borrowing beyond what the primary surplus requires is inflationary pressure on the economy — compressing the real purchasing power of wages and savings. Digital campaigns that make this borrowing look like an opportunity for citizens are a distraction from the scale of the structural problem.
