Pakistan Public Debt · June 2026

The T-bill "investment" illusion:
who pays and who profits

On June 16 2026, VEON's JazzCash and Mobilink Bank announced Pakistan's government treasury bills are now available via smartphone at ₨5,000 minimum — calling it "democratising investment for millions of Pakistanis." The official SBP and Ministry of Finance data tells a different story.

The cruel arithmetic: Pakistan achieved a primary surplus of ₨1.8 trillion in FY-25 — meaning revenues exceeded non-interest spending. Yet debt still grew 13% because interest payments of ₨9.46 trillion devoured it more than 5 times over. The country is running faster just to stay in place. Then the government rolled ₨2 trillion of short-term T-bills into long-term PIBs — burden deferred, not resolved.

The primary dealer system: T-bill auctions in Pakistan are conducted through primary dealers — commercial banks that bid directly with the government in a closed room, locking in institutional rates. The public is excluded from this process. JazzCash gives retail customers secondary-market access after banks have already priced the instrument. This is not democratising investment. It is adding a fintech fee layer on top of a bank-dominated system that has not changed.

What the launch actually provides: A NASDAQ-listed multinational (VEON) earns a fee on Pakistani citizens buying government debt instruments — after commercial banks have set the pricing at closed auctions. NSS (National Savings Schemes) has offered retail access to government securities for decades. JazzCash adds a smartphone interface, a mandatory Mobilink Bank account requirement, and a press conference with the Finance Minister. It does not change who holds the debt, who sets the rates, or who ultimately bears the interest burden. The public doesn't invest in T-bills. It pays for them.

The can-kicking cycle

₨7.1T FY-25. Revenues can't cover spending
Issue T-bills
Banks buy at closed primary auction. Public excluded from pricing
Roll over to PIBs
₨2T T-bills retired, ₨12T PIBs issued. Avg maturity 2.8 → 3.8 yrs
Public bears cost
₨9.46T interest via taxes & electricity surcharges. Debt still +13%

On the maturity "improvement": The Annual Debt Review FY-25 celebrates extending average time to maturity from 2.8 to 3.8 years as "reducing rollover risk." What this means is the government has locked citizens into paying interest for longer — T-bill debt repackaged as long-term PIBs and Sukuks. The can has been kicked further down the road, not picked up.
